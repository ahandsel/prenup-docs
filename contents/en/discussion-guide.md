# {{ $frontmatter.title }}

Marriage joins two lives and two sets of expectations.
This guide helps you surface those expectations early, while they are easy to shape.

You get the topics that matter most, open-ended questions to ask each other, and a way to turn your answers into written agreements.

If you later sign a prenuptial agreement (a contract a couple signs before marriage that sets out how certain matters are handled), this work becomes its foundation.

This guide is educational, not legal advice.
Local law varies, so consult a qualified professional before finalizing any agreement.

## How to use this guide 🧭

These conversations are meant to bring you closer, not to grade each other.
A few habits help:

* **Set aside unhurried time.** Pick a calm moment. Some topics need more than one sitting.
* **Answer separately, then compare.** Write your own answers first. This surfaces honest differences instead of one person echoing the other.
* **Treat differences as information.** The goal is not to agree on everything. It is to know where you align, where you differ, and how you will handle the gaps.

When you reach a clear understanding, write it down in plain language.
A shared note beats a vague memory of having "talked about it once."


## Finances and income 💰

How you handle money day to day shapes how smoothly married life runs.
Talking openly about earnings and spending prevents small habits from growing into resentment.


Questions to ask each other:

* How will we handle our incomes: keep separate accounts, combine everything into joint accounts, or use a mix of both?
* Who will be responsible for which everyday expenses, such as rent or mortgage, utilities, groceries, and insurance?
* How will we divide shared costs if our incomes are very different from each other?
* What is the largest amount either of us can spend without checking in with the other first?
* How will we make decisions when we disagree about a purchase?
* How much do we each want to keep in savings to feel secure, and what are we saving toward?


Translate answers into agreements ✍️:

Move from "we will share money" to a specific arrangement.
For example: each partner pays into a joint account in proportion to income, the joint account covers a named list of expenses, and any purchase above a set amount needs a conversation first.
Writing down who pays what, into which account, and for which expenses gives a prenuptial agreement something concrete to capture.


## Property and debt 🏠

Each of you arrives with things you own and, often, money you owe.
Marriage can blur the line between what is yours and what is shared, so decide early how to treat what you bring in and what you build together.
Full, honest disclosure of debts matters most: a hidden debt can become a shared burden.


Questions to ask each other:

* What does each of us own now, and what debts does each of us carry, including loans, credit cards, and any obligations to family?
* Should the assets each of us brings into the marriage stay separate, or become shared?
* How do we want to treat property and savings we build up together after we marry?
* If one of us runs into financial trouble, do we expect the other to help cover it?
* How will we handle any new debt we take on as a couple?


Translate answers into agreements ✍️:

First, define your terms.
* *Separate property* usually means assets one partner owned before marriage or keeps in their own name.
* *Shared property* (sometimes called marital or community property) usually means assets the couple builds up during the marriage.

Exact definitions depend on local law, so confirm them with a qualified professional.

Then each partner can list what they own and owe, with rough values, and state in plain language how each item should be treated.
For example: "the apartment one partner owned before marriage stays that partner's separate property."
These lists are exactly what a prenuptial agreement attaches and formalizes.


## Family and children 🚸

Few decisions shape a marriage more than whether to raise children and how to care for them.
Partners often assume they agree here and find out too late that they do not.
Be direct and honest rather than saying what you think the other wants to hear.


Questions to ask each other:

* Do we want to have children? If so, how many, and roughly when?
* If having children naturally is not possible, how do we feel about options such as adoption or fertility treatment?
* How do we imagine sharing day-to-day caregiving and the everyday work of raising a child?
* If either of us already has children from a previous relationship, how will we care for and provide for them?
* How will we cover the costs of raising children, from daily needs to schooling?
* What role do we want extended family, such as grandparents, to play?

Translate answers into agreements ✍️:

Translate hopes into specifics.
Instead of "we both want kids someday," record when you plan to start a family, how you will share caregiving, and how you will split major costs such as childcare and education.
Where one partner has children from a prior relationship, note how their needs will be honored.

A prenuptial agreement can record how child-related expenses are handled between the two of you.
But child custody and child support are usually decided under local law in the child's interest, not by private contract, so confirm this with a qualified professional.


## Values, goals, and lifestyle 🌱

Beneath daily logistics lie deeper questions about the life each of you wants.
Careers, where you live, faith, and long-term dreams all shape the marriage.
Partners who understand each other's direction can plan as a team.


Questions to ask each other:

* What are each of our career ambitions, and how do we want to balance work with the rest of our lives?
* Where do we want to live, and what would make either of us willing to relocate?
* How important is religion or spirituality to each of us, and how do we want it to shape our home and any children's upbringing?
* What does a meaningful life look like to each of us, and how do we picture ourselves ten years from now?
* How much independence does each of us want to keep, in terms of friendships, hobbies, and time alone?
* Whose surname will each of us use, and what name will any children have?


Translate answers into agreements ✍️:

Some answers are about direction, and simply naming them aloud is valuable.
Others can become concrete commitments.
For example: whose career takes priority if a job requires a move, or how you will support a partner who pauses paid work to study or care for family.
Where a choice has financial consequences, note how that will be recognized.
This is the kind of understanding a prenuptial agreement can protect.


## Conflict, communication, and the relationship 💬

Every couple disagrees.
What separates strong marriages from struggling ones is how partners handle conflict, not its absence.
Understand how each of you was raised to deal with disagreement, and what you each need when tensions rise.


Questions to ask each other:

* How were you each raised to handle disagreement: openly, quietly, or with conflict?
* How do you each tend to respond when you are angry, sad, or frustrated, and what helps you feel heard?
* How will we make big decisions together, and how will we settle things when we cannot agree?
* How often would it help to check in with each other about how the relationship is going?
* What do we each expect from the other in hard times, such as illness, job loss, or family stress?
* What do we most admire about each other, and what habits test our patience?

Translate answers into agreements ✍️:

Most of this does not belong in a legal document.
The value is the shared understanding itself.
For example: either of you can ask for a short pause during a heated argument, or you will hold a regular check-in.
Write these down as simple relationship agreements you both keep.
They rarely belong in a prenuptial agreement, but they strengthen the partnership it is meant to support.


## Formalizing the understanding 📝

Once you have talked through the topics above and written down your decisions, you may want to give some of them legal weight.
This is the optional final step, where a prenuptial agreement comes in.

A prenuptial agreement is a written contract a couple signs before marriage.
It records how the couple treats property and finances: which assets stay separate, how shared property is defined, and what happens to certain assets if the marriage ends or a partner dies.
It is the formal version of the financial conversations you have already had.

There are limits, and they vary by location:

* A prenuptial agreement suits financial and property matters between the two partners.
* It is not the place to settle child custody or child support, which local law usually decides in the child's interest.
* The rules for making it valid and enforceable differ by jurisdiction.

For these reasons, involve a qualified professional who knows your local law.
They can confirm what the agreement can cover, prepare it correctly, and check that both partners have given honest financial disclosure.


Translate answers into agreements ✍️:

Gather the notes and lists you made along the way, especially each partner's list of property and debts with rough values.
Decide together what the agreement should cover: how separate and shared property are defined, how expenses are handled, and what happens to specific assets if the marriage ends.
Arriving with this material turns drafting from a blank page into putting decisions you have already made into proper form.


## Next steps and a note on legal advice ⚖️

* Pick two or three topics above and schedule your first conversation.
* Answer separately, compare, and write down what you agree on in plain language.
* Keep a running document of your shared understandings and each partner's list of property and debts.
* When you are ready to formalize anything, take that document to a qualified professional who knows your local law.

This guide is for education and reflection only.
It is not legal advice, and nothing here replaces guidance from a qualified professional.
Laws on marriage, property, debt, children, and prenuptial agreements vary by jurisdiction and change over time.
Before signing any agreement, both partners should seek independent, professional advice.
